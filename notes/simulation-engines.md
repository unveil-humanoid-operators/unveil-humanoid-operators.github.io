# Robot-simulation engines — notes & comparison

Author's note: a personal knowledge book on robot simulation engines, organized around
**how this project uses (or doesn't use) them** and **what you'd reach for if you wanted
to do something other than what we do here**. Aimed at the Unitree G1 humanoid, since
that's what the UNVEIL paper / website is built around.

---

## 1. TL;DR — what does *this* project actually use?

| Layer in our pipeline                                                              | Engine / tool                                                                                                                                                                                                                       | Where it lives                                                                       |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Robot kinematic model** (link tree, joint axes, mesh attachments, joint limits)  | **MuJoCo MJCF** (`<mujoco model="g1_29dof">`) — the same XML schema as Google DeepMind's `mujoco_menagerie`, with Unitree damping/armature/friction defaults                                                                         | `g1_engine/model/g1_29dof.xml`                                                       |
| **Joint-angle trajectories (CSV)** — the dataset rows we play back                 | Produced upstream by **NVIDIA's MuJoCo-based G1 retargeter** (NVIDIA SOMA → G1 kinematic IK). Column-named `move_g1_mujoco_path` in `seed_metadata_v003.parquet`. We do not run this retargeter; we consume its output.              | `bones-seed/g1/csv/{date}/{motion}.csv`                                              |
| **Browser playback / 3-D rendering** of those trajectories                         | **Three.js r128** (kinematic forward kinematics only — *no physics, no contacts, no controller*). Reads our XML once offline → flat JSON; then per-frame writes `quaternion = setFromAxisAngle(joint.axis, angleRad)` for each body. | `g1_engine/viewer.html`, `g1_engine/generate_model.py`                               |
| **Human-mesh playback** (the SMPL row in our demo blocks and feature-leak matrix)  | Vanilla Three.js again, but with **pre-baked per-frame SMPL vertex positions** (no SMPL skinning at runtime). Produced offline by `BVH2SMPL` + VPoser.                                                                               | `smpl_engine/viewer.html`, `assets/demos/generate_all_demos.py`                      |
| **Physics simulation at runtime**                                                  | **None.** We never integrate contact forces. The robot looks like it's "walking" because the *recorded* joint angles already encode the gait — we're just driving the kinematic tree from a table.                                  | —                                                                                    |

So when someone asks *"what simulator does UNVEIL use?"* the honest, pedantic answer is:

> The G1 robot model comes from the MuJoCo MJCF ecosystem, and the trajectories were
> generated by an upstream NVIDIA MuJoCo-based retargeter. The website itself runs
> **zero physics** — it's a kinematic player built on Three.js, deliberately so, because
> the paper claims that biometric leakage is *already present in the recorded joint
> angles*, before any physics is run on them.

The "before any physics" framing is load-bearing: a reviewer could otherwise ask whether
the leakage is an artifact of a particular contact solver, and the answer is *no — the
leak is in the input kinematics*.

## 2. Why not Isaac Sim / Gazebo / PyBullet / Genesis for *our* site?

Because the website's job is *visualization of fixed sequences*, not *control* or
*training*. Once you commit to "the joint angles are what they are", a physics engine
buys you nothing and costs you a lot:

1. **GPU + WebGPU overhead.** Isaac Sim won't run in a tab; even MJX needs WebAssembly
   plumbing that doubles the page weight.
2. **Determinism and reproducibility.** Two visitors must see exactly the same playback.
   Any physics engine introduces solver-tolerance noise and contact-resolution drift.
3. **Tracked authorship.** The dataset's exact joint-angle CSVs are the artifact we want
   reviewers to inspect. A physics layer would invite the question *"are you showing the
   real CSV or what the simulator produced from it?"*

For everything *outside the website* — training new policies, sim-to-real, dataset
augmentation — we'd reach for the engines below.

## 3. Engine-by-engine reference

### 3.1 MuJoCo (Multi-Joint dynamics with Contact)
Originally by Emo Todorov; open-sourced by Google DeepMind in 2021.

- **Model format**: MJCF (a richer XML than URDF; native to MuJoCo). URDF can be
  imported. Our `g1_29dof.xml` is MJCF.
- **Strengths**:
  - **Best-in-class soft contact and constraint solver** for articulated bodies — the
    standard for academic locomotion / manipulation research for years.
  - Headless or with a GLFW viewer; Python, C, MATLAB bindings.
  - **Fast on CPU**: tens of kHz step rate for a humanoid; stable up to ~3 ms timesteps.
  - **Differentiable variant: MJX** (JAX/XLA) — JIT-compiled to GPU/TPU; 10–50× faster
    throughput than vanilla MuJoCo for batched rollouts. Used as the physics backend by
    Brax and MuJoCo Playground; trained Unitree-Go1 locomotion in ~minutes on a single
    RTX 4090.
- **Weaknesses**:
  - No first-class sensor / ROS story (compared to Gazebo).
  - Vanilla MuJoCo is single-rollout — for massive parallelism you need MJX (which means
    JAX).
- **Used by**: pretty much every modern humanoid-locomotion paper, OpenAI's old Gym-MuJoCo
  benchmarks, NVIDIA's retargeting pipeline, DeepMind Control Suite.
- **Pointer**: `mujoco_menagerie/unitree_g1` is the maintained MJCF (a *simplified*
  Unitree G1 with PD-tuned actuators in an MJX variant validated on real hardware).

### 3.2 NVIDIA Isaac Sim / Isaac Lab
- **Isaac Sim** = standalone simulator built on **PhysX 5** + **USD** for scene
  composition + Omniverse RTX renderer.
- **Isaac Lab** = the official RL framework on top of Isaac Sim (replaces Isaac Gym Envs).
- **Strengths**:
  - **GPU-parallel rollouts** at extreme scale — 4096–16384 envs per GPU is normal.
  - **Photorealistic rendering** (RTX path tracing) → useful when your policy is
    vision-conditioned and you care about sim-to-real visual gap.
  - **USD scene graph**: easy to compose multi-robot, multi-asset scenes; integrates with
    NVIDIA's GR00T humanoid foundation-model stack.
  - **Isaac Lab 2.3** (Nov 2025) added **whole-body control + teleop for Unitree G1**
    natively, with dexterous retargeting for the G1 three-finger and Inspire five-finger
    hands.
  - **Unitree-official integration**: `unitree_sim_isaaclab` (uses the same DDS messages
    as the physical robot) and `unitree_rl_lab` (RL trainer for Go2/H1/G1-29dof).
- **Weaknesses**:
  - Heavyweight: NVIDIA RTX GPU required; multi-GB install; OS support narrower than
    MuJoCo (Linux strongly preferred).
  - Slower to iterate on a single config because of the USD/Omniverse layer.
  - PhysX is less paper-cited for contact-rich academic research than MuJoCo — though
    that gap is closing.
- **Use it when**: training a humanoid locomotion or whole-body manipulation policy at
  scale, especially if you're targeting NVIDIA-hosted deployment (Jetson Orin on real G1)
  or using GR00T-style models.

### 3.3 PyBullet (Bullet3 Python bindings)
- **Engine**: Bullet rigid-body engine, Featherstone articulated bodies by default.
- **Strengths**:
  - Free, pure-Python install, **easy onboarding**, well-documented.
  - Built-in IK, ID, VR, and headless modes.
  - The default for many imitation-learning / robosuite older codebases.
- **Weaknesses**:
  - Contact stability lags MuJoCo (stable up to ~7 ms timesteps in benchmarks vs 3 ms for
    MuJoCo).
  - No GPU parallelism story (the Bullet GPU broadphase exists but isn't used in PyBullet
    RL).
- **Use it when**: you want the shortest path from `pip install` to a running humanoid in
  a notebook, and you don't need GPU-scale rollouts.

### 3.4 Gazebo (Classic + Gazebo Sim, fka Ignition)
- **Strengths**:
  - **ROS / ROS 2 first**: SDF model files, sensor plugins (cameras, IMU, lidar),
    multi-robot scenes, navigation-stack integration. The default for robotics integration
    testing.
  - Used for almost every robotics competition that wants reproducible field-robot setups.
- **Weaknesses**:
  - Physics backend (ODE/DART) is less precise for contact-rich manipulation than MuJoCo.
  - Heavier to run; slower for high-volume RL rollouts.
- **Use it when**: you're integrating with a real ROS 2 stack and the simulator is
  primarily a stand-in for the *whole system* (sensors, controllers, motion planning),
  not just the robot dynamics.

### 3.5 Drake (TRI / MIT)
- **Strengths**:
  - **Planning, optimization, and controls-first** simulator: multibody dynamics with
    high-fidelity hydroelastic contact, trajectory optimization, MPC, system-identification
    primitives baked in.
  - First-class C++ and Python, with rigorous units / typed numerics.
- **Weaknesses**:
  - Steeper learning curve; less of a drop-in for "I just want to train PPO."
  - Smaller community for humanoid-locomotion RL than MuJoCo / Isaac.
- **Use it when**: you're doing model-based control, contact-aware trajectory
  optimization, or you need provable system identification (Drake is the academic
  reference for hydroelastic contact).

### 3.6 Genesis (Genesis-Embodied-AI)
- **Strengths**:
  - **Multi-physics**: rigid body + FEM + MPM + particle (PBD/SPH) + uipc, all sharing
    one scene and one state.
  - Pure-Python, GPU-accelerated. Marketed as **"up to 430,000× real-time on RTX 4090"**
    for some workloads — caveat that the benchmark is engine-specific and not all
    humanoid-RL pipelines see that speedup yet.
  - Photoreal renderer (Nyx); cross-platform compiler (Quadrants).
  - **Genesis World 1.0** released May 2026 with explicit "sim-to-real as a compute
    problem" pitch.
- **Weaknesses**:
  - Younger than MuJoCo/Isaac → fewer canonical humanoid baselines, smaller paper
    citation count.
  - The "430,000×" headline number isn't humanoid-typical — read the small print.
- **Use it when**: you need *coupled* multiphysics (soft body + rigid + fluid) in one
  scene, or you're a Python-first lab and don't want JAX (MJX) or USD (Isaac).

### 3.7 unitree_mujoco (Unitree's official MuJoCo wrapper)
- **What it is**: thin wrapper around MuJoCo that exposes the G1 (also H1, Go2) over the
  **Unitree SDK2 DDS** interface — same DDS messages as the real robot.
- **Why it matters**: you can write a controller against `unitree_g1.py` and run it
  against either the simulator or the real robot **without changing application code**.
  Runs at 500 Hz (2 ms timestep) by default.
- **Companion stack**:
  - `unitree_rl_lab` (Isaac Lab–based RL training).
  - `unitree_rl_mjlab` (MuJoCo-based RL training, combines Isaac Lab's API with MuJoCo's
    physics — lighter and more portable than full Isaac).
  - `unitree_sim_isaaclab` (Isaac Lab sim that also speaks DDS).
- **Use it when**: developing controllers or policies you intend to deploy to a physical
  G1 — eliminates an entire class of "but it worked in sim!" surprises tied to interface
  mismatch.

### 3.8 Three.js (what *we* use for the site)
- Not a physics engine — a WebGL scene graph. Listed here because it's where our
  end-user experience actually lives, and because there's a small ecosystem of robot
  visualization tools built on it (Robosynx, URDF-Studio, urdf-loader). All of them
  load URDF/MJCF/STL and play back joint angles; none of them solve dynamics.
- **Use it when**: you want a robot to appear in a web page or paper-companion site, with
  zero install cost for the visitor.

## 4. Specifically: simulating a Unitree G1 trajectory

A "G1 trajectory" can mean several different things. Pick the matching tool:

### 4.1 *Play back* a pre-recorded joint-angle trajectory in a browser
(What this project does.)

- **Engine**: Three.js + the G1 MJCF parsed offline into JSON.
- **Why this and not MuJoCo-in-browser**: zero physics needed; the CSV already encodes
  the gait; we want determinism, low install cost (none, it's a tab), and the ability to
  swap activities via a dropdown.
- **Alternatives**: Robosynx (drag-and-drop URDF/MJCF visualization), URDF-Studio (more
  authoring focus). Both would work but lock us into their UI and lose the per-block
  sync controller we built.

### 4.2 Play back the same trajectory *in MuJoCo* (sanity-check / debug)
- **Engine**: standalone `mujoco` Python bindings + `mujoco_menagerie/unitree_g1/g1.xml`.
- **Workflow**: `mj_step` with the recorded joint angles either as setpoints (PD-track)
  or as direct `data.qpos` writes (kinematic). Useful if you want to see *what the gait
  looks like with contacts enforced* — the trajectory was retargeted assuming a free-fly
  root, so contact-enforced playback will often fall over. That's expected and not a bug.
- **Why bother**: confirms the CSV units (cm + degrees in our case, see CLAUDE.md) and
  the root-Euler order (ZYX) you've assumed are correct. We can recommend `unitree_mujoco`
  if you also want to test through DDS.

### 4.3 *Train a new policy* that produces G1 trajectories
- **Best default**: **MJX** (MuJoCo-XLA) on a single RTX 4090 — fast, JAX-native, well
  tested with the G1 menagerie model. ~minutes-to-hours for a locomotion baseline at
  8192 parallel envs.
- **Best for scale / GR00T integration**: **Isaac Lab** + `unitree_rl_lab`. Heavier
  setup but tightly integrated with NVIDIA's humanoid-foundation-model stack and Jetson
  deployment path.
- **Best for fastest "hello world"**: **PyBullet** + a Gym wrapper. Lower ceiling, but a
  single afternoon to standing-balance.

### 4.4 *Retarget* a new BVH motion to G1 yourself
- The community-favorite for 2026 is **GMR (General Motion Retargeting)** — Yanjie Ze,
  ICRA 2026 — CPU, real-time, MuJoCo-validated. NVIDIA's internal retargeter (the one
  BONES-SEED used) is not publicly released; GMR is the closest open analogue.
- **Standard recipe**: pick corresponding joints → align local axes → height-ratio
  scale → IK in two stages (root pose, then per-joint) → MuJoCo forward kinematics to
  check self-collision.

### 4.5 *Sim-to-real* on a physical G1
- **Stack**: train in **Isaac Lab** or **MJX with domain randomization** → export policy
  → deploy via **`unitree_sdk2`** to the Jetson Orin on the robot. `unitree_mujoco`'s
  DDS interface makes this almost a no-op on the application side.

## 5. Trade-off cheat-sheet

| Need                                    | Reach for                       | Why                                                                                                          |
| --------------------------------------- | ------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Browser-side viz of a recorded motion   | Three.js + MJCF→JSON            | Zero install, deterministic, no physics drift.                                                               |
| Single-machine RL on a humanoid         | MJX (MuJoCo-XLA) via Brax       | Best research-grade physics + JAX-native batch rollouts. Single RTX 4090 covers most labs.                   |
| Industrial-scale RL + pixel observations| Isaac Lab                       | GPU rollouts at 10k+ envs and PathTracer for vision-conditioned policies.                                    |
| Sim-to-real on a Unitree robot          | `unitree_mujoco` + Isaac Lab    | DDS interface is identical to the real robot, removes a whole class of integration bugs.                     |
| ROS 2 system testing (cameras, planner) | Gazebo Sim                      | SDF + first-class sensor plugins + ROS topics; the only one of these designed for it.                        |
| Model-based control / trajectory opt    | Drake                           | Hydroelastic contact, system identification, MPC and planning primitives are built-in.                       |
| Multiphysics in one scene               | Genesis                         | Coupled rigid + soft + fluid in a Python-native engine.                                                      |
| Easiest "hello, humanoid"               | PyBullet                        | `pip install`, no GPU needed, lots of tutorials.                                                             |
| Retarget your own mocap to G1           | GMR + MuJoCo for validation     | Open, MuJoCo-validated, real-time on CPU.                                                                    |

## 6. Footnotes & honest caveats

- **MuJoCo vs PhysX accuracy debate**: the well-cited Erez/Tassa benchmarks are old
  (2015). Modern Isaac Sim's PhysX is much better than the version those papers measured.
  Pick on ecosystem fit, not just on solver papers.
- **Genesis numbers**: take the "430,000× real-time" headline with salt — that's a
  specific engine-internal benchmark; humanoid PPO doesn't get close to that.
- **MJX vs Isaac Lab is not always "MJX wins on speed"** for humanoid RL — Isaac Lab on a
  high-VRAM GPU with 16k envs can outpace MJX on a 24 GB 4090 once you're vision-
  conditioned and the renderer matters.
- **"Sim-to-real gap" for the G1 is comparatively small** (the consensus in 2025–26
  community write-ups) because the robot is light (~35 kg) with tight joint dynamics —
  domain randomization that would fail on a 100 kg humanoid often transfers cleanly on G1.
